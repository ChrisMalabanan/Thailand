Thailand — Land of Smiles

Thailand is a Southeast Asian country known for tropical beaches, opulent royal palaces, ancient ruins, and ornate temples displaying figures of Buddha. In Bangkok, the capital, an ultramodern cityscape rises beside quiet canalside communities and the iconic temples of Wat Arun, Wat Pho, and the Emerald Buddha Temple (Wat Phra Kaew).

It's the “Land of Smiles” and “Land of the Free”: Thailand is known for its friendly people and is the only Southeast Asian country never colonized by Europeans. Its name, “Prathet Thai,” translates to “land of the free.”

Thai Cuisine

Known for its balance of sweet, spicy, sour, and salty flavors, Thai cuisine reflects the rich culinary traditions of Thailand.

Mango Sticky Rice

A traditional Thai dessert made with glutinous rice, fresh mango slices, and coconut milk. The sticky rice is cooked with coconut milk and sugar, giving it a rich and creamy texture that pairs perfectly with the sweet and juicy mango.

Pad Thai

A stir-fried rice noodle dish commonly served as street food and at casual local eateries in Thailand. It typically includes ingredients like shrimp, tofu, peanuts, scrambled egg, and bean sprouts, all flavored with tamarind pulp, fish sauce, dried shrimp, garlic, red chili pepper, and palm sugar.

Tom Yum Goong

A hot and sour Thai soup usually cooked with shrimp. The soup is characterized by its distinct hot and sour flavors, with fragrant spices and herbs generously used in the broth,including lemongrass.

Cultural Highlights

Thailand boasts a rich cultural heritage with vibrant festivals, traditional dance, and intricate craftsmanship.

Loy-Krathong Festival

Celebrated annually on the full moon night of the 12th lunar month, this festival involves floating decorated baskets (krathongs) on rivers and lakes to pay respect to the water goddess, cultural performances like traditional music and dance, beauty pageants, and, in some regions like Chiang Mai, releasing sky lanterns (which is part of the related Yi Peng festival).

Yi Peng Lantern Festival

Celebrated in northern Thailand, particularly in Chiang Mai, this festival features the release of thousands of paper lanterns into the sky, symbolizing the letting go of misfortunes and making wishes for the future. Today, Yi Peng has evolved into a large cultural event that includes parades, traditional music, dancing, and other performances, in addition to the lantern releases.

Songkran Festival

Celebrated as the Thai New Year in April, this festival is famous for its water fights, where people splash water on each other as a symbol of cleansing and renewal. The word "Songkran" comes from Sanskrit and means "to move forward" or "to pass," symbolizing a fresh start. Beyond the public water fights, ancient rituals like gently pouring water on Buddha images are performed to wash away misfortune and bring good luck for the new year.
